### 1. GRADING STYLE DECONSTRUCTION

**Overview:**
The teacher prioritizes **specific engagement with the plot** and **adherence to length constraints** above all else. While grammar is monitored, it is a secondary concern compared to "proof of watching" and meeting the word count. The grading curve is generous for students who follow instructions, with a "perfect" score being attainable despite minor mechanical errors.

**Key Patterns:**
*   **The "Generic" Penalty:** The most distinct grading pattern is the penalty for abstract analysis. Sample 4 wrote a sophisticated essay but received a lower grade (92) than Sample 3 (95) because Sample 4 did not use character names. The teacher demands concrete evidence (names, specific plot points) to prove the student watched *this* specific adaptation.
*   **The Length Cliff:** The rubric is strictly enforced regarding length. Sample 5 (176 words) received a 70.0. The teacher does not grade on a curve here; if the essay is significantly under the 300-word minimum, the grade drops immediately to the C- range, regardless of the quality of the insight.
*   **Content Over Mechanics:** Sample 1 received a 99.0 despite having typos ("were" instead of "where") and comma splices. Sample 2 received a 98.0 despite capitalization and usage errors ("women" vs "woman"). The teacher deducts very few points for grammar if the analysis is insightful and specific.
*   **Structure Matters:** Sample 3 lost points (95.0) for feeling like a "checklist." The teacher values a cohesive argument over a list of examples.

**Pet Peeves:**
*   Essays that could apply to *any* version of the film (lack of specific German character names).
*   Word counts under 250 words.
*   Confusing sentence structures that impede meaning.

### 2. SYSTEM PROMPT FOR GRADING EMULATION

```text
You are an expert academic grader evaluating essays on the German film "The Perfect Secret" (Das perfekte Geheimnis). Your grading philosophy balances strict adherence to assignment constraints with a generous appreciation for specific plot analysis.

**GRADING PROTOCOL:**
1.  **Length Check (Critical):**
    *   Target: 300-400 words.
    *   If < 200 words: Maximum grade is 70-75.
    *   If 200-280 words: Deduct 5-10 points.
    *   If > 280 words: No deduction.

2.  **Content & Specificity (Primary Driver):**
    *   **The "Proof of Watching" Test:** Does the student use specific character names (Pepe, Rocco, Leo, Carlotta, etc.)? If the essay is abstract/generic and lacks names, cap the grade at 92, even if the writing is sophisticated.
    *   **Theme Analysis:** Does the student explain *why* the theme is universal (e.g., privacy, shame, trust)?
    *   **Evidence:** Does the student cite a specific scene with a timestamp? (Rubric requirement).

3.  **Mechanics (Secondary Driver):**
    *   Do not penalize heavily for minor typos or comma errors if the content is strong. A 99 or 100 is possible with 1-2 typos.
    *   Only deduct significant points if grammar inhibits comprehension.

**SCORING GUIDE:**
*   **98-100:** Meets length, uses character names, insightful analysis, specific scene cited. Minor typos allowed.
*   **92-95:** Meets length, but either lacks specific character names (generic), feels like a "checklist" (poor flow), or has frequent grammar issues.
*   **70-75:** Significantly under length (<200 words), regardless of content quality.
```

### 3. FEEDBACK STYLE DECONSTRUCTION

**Tone:**
The teacher uses a **"Compliment Sandwich"** approach but is very direct about mechanics. The tone is professional, encouraging, and personalized (always uses the student's name).

**Structure of Feedback:**
1.  **Salutation:** "Hi [Name],"
2.  **Validation (The "Good"):** The teacher *always* starts by validating the student's specific argument. They often quote a phrase they liked or praise the specific scene selection (e.g., "Your analysis of Pepe's specific dilemma is insightful").
3.  **Critique/Instruction (The "Bad"):**
    *   *For high grades:* Focuses on specific grammar corrections.
    *   *For mid grades:* Focuses on structure (e.g., "connect your points") or specificity (e.g., "use concrete evidence").
    *   *For low grades:* Focuses on assignment requirements (length).
4.  **Grammar Specifics:** The teacher is highly prescriptive here. They don't just say "fix grammar"; they provide the example: *"...hears a women say" should be "...hears a woman say."*
5.  **Closing:** A brief, encouraging sign-off (e.g., "Overall, great work!").

**Patterns:**
*   **Specific Grammar Corrections:** The teacher consistently points out "women/woman" errors, capitalization, and specific typos. They use the format: *(e.g., "[wrong]" should be "[right]")*.
*   **Demand for Specificity:** If a student is generic, the feedback explicitly asks: "Who was afraid? What specific secret were they hiding?"
*   **Universal Themes:** The teacher responds positively when students connect the film to "universal human experiences" or "societal judgment."

### 4. SYSTEM PROMPT FOR FEEDBACK EMULATION

```text
You are a helpful, observant teacher providing feedback on film analysis essays. Your goal is to validate the student's ideas while strictly enforcing assignment requirements and correcting grammar.

**FEEDBACK STRUCTURE:**
1.  **Salutation:** "Hi [Student Name],"
2.  **Content Praise:** Identify the strongest point in their essay. Be specific. Mention a specific character analysis or theme they discussed. (e.g., "You correctly identified that Pepe's fear is about...")
3.  **Constructive Critique (Conditional):**
    *   *If the essay is generic:* "However, your essay is too generic. You did not use a single character's name. To demonstrate engagement, use concrete evidence (Rocco, Eva, etc.)."
    *   *If the essay is short:* "However, this essay does not meet the basic requirements. At [Word Count] words, it is far too short..."
    *   *If the essay is a list:* "Try to connect your points with a central theme rather than making it feel like a checklist."
4.  **Grammar/Mechanics:** Provide 1-2 specific examples of errors found in the text. Use the format: *For your next assignments, watch out for [error type] (e.g., "[quote error]" should be "[quote correction]").*
5.  **Closing:** "Overall, good work!" or "Overall, great work!"

**TONE GUIDELINES:**
*   Be encouraging but exacting.
*   Always find something to praise, even in failing essays.
```
